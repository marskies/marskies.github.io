Overview

Cosmic Catch puts you in the role of a pop star mermaid, using your musical powers to catch celestial fish and add them to your collection. The concept merges the cozy satisfaction of fishing games with rhythm-based mechanics‚Äîtiming your inputs to the beat to reel in your catch.

Created during a 12-hour Create-A-Thon with the theme "Star," our team of five developed a cohesive vision that earned the Most Innovative award. While programming challenges prevented a working prototype, the strength of our concept, art direction, and presentation demonstrated what a polished vision can achieve even without functional mechanics.

Final Piece

Our deliverable was a comprehensive concept package rather than a playable prototype. We presented a complete vision of what Cosmic Catch could be: a meditative fishing experience where players learn the rhythms of different cosmic fish, building their collection while enjoying an ambient soundtrack.

The package included fully realized art assets‚Äîour mermaid protagonist, three unique fish designs with animation poses, the space moon pool environment, and a complete UI system. We also delivered three original "fish fight" music tracks with percussion and vocals, plus ambient background music. The judges saw not just an idea, but a world ready to be built.

Process

Our initial brainstorm landed on a tight concept: rhythm-based fishing with a star theme. We scoped it small on purpose. But passion has a way of expanding scope‚Äîone teammate's enthusiasm for adding a collection system was hard to resist, and suddenly we had two interconnected systems instead of one focused mechanic.

With four hours left and programming hitting unfamiliar technical walls, I had to make a call. I assessed where we actually were versus where we hoped to be, and redirected the team's energy. Instead of forcing a broken prototype, we would highlight everything that was done and present a polished concept. We focused on what we had‚Äîincredible art, cohesive direction, original music‚Äîrather than scrambling to ship buggy mechanics.

That pivot meant packaging our vision in a way judges could understand and get excited about. The result was a presentation that showed not just what we made, but what Cosmic Catch could become‚Äîand that vision won Most Innovative.

What I Learned

Sometimes you can pick your team, and sometimes you can't. This jam taught me that either way, understanding your teammates' actual capabilities before scoping is essential. We asked our programmer to tackle unfamiliar systems without fully understanding the learning curve involved. Going forward, I'd interview teammates about their comfort zones and experience levels before committing to technical scope.

The bigger lesson: always build on a foundation. Establish your bare minimum first‚Äîthe smallest version that proves the concept works‚Äîthen layer on features. We tried to build both the rhythm system and collection system simultaneously, and ended up with neither. A working rhythm demo with placeholder art would have been stronger than beautiful art with no mechanics.

But here's the flip side: a polished partial vision can still win. Knowing when to pivot from "ship the prototype" to "sell the concept" is its own skill. Sometimes the best project management decision is recognizing what you actually have and presenting it with confidence.
