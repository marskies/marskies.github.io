Introduction

[Mermaid Character:
Main Sprite]

Cosmic Catch began with a simple question: what if fishing met rhythm gameplay in the coziest way possible? During a 10-hour Create-A-Thon, our team of five set out to merge our shared love of fishing and music into something unique and memorable.

The concept featured a mermaid protagonist catching fish to the beat, creating a meditative yet engaging experience. While the technical execution faced significant challenges, the originality of combining these genres in a cozy context earned us the Most Innovative award.

[UI Mockup:
Gameplay Screen]

As Project Manager and UI/UX Designer, I wore multiple hats: designing the gameplay loop UX, creating UI art assets, managing scope under extreme time pressure, and ultimately orchestrating our pivot when programming complications arose. This project became a masterclass in adaptive leadership and creative problem-solving.

The Concept

[Fish Collection:
Various Designs]

Our vision was deliberately cozy rather than competitive. The mermaid would swim through cosmic waters, timing button presses to catch colorful fish flowing past. We designed it as a simple collection game‚Äîscope-conscious from the start, knowing we had only 10 hours.

Anna Kubart brought the world to life with enchanting character art and fish designs. Aaron Snodgrass composed three original tracks plus background music, each building the rhythmic foundation. I designed the main gameplay UX and created all UI elements, from the collection screen interface to button states and transitions.

[Art Assets:
Background Elements]

The team dynamic was collaborative, with everyone contributing to design discussions. I documented everything in our Game Design Document, locking in decisions quickly so we could move forward without second-guessing. With Warrick Brugal and Cameron Caangay handling programming, we thought we had all the pieces in place.

The Challenge

[Team Workspace:
Create-A-Thon Day]

Reality hit hard around hour six. While all art assets were complete and music tracks were polished, programming implementation stalled. Our team's limited Unity experience meant even basic rhythm mechanics proved complex under time pressure. By hour eight, we had only achieved scene transitions‚Äîtitle screen to gameplay and back.

This is where project management became crucial. I made the difficult call to pivot from "shipping a playable game" to "presenting a compelling concept." I directed Aaron to package his music as a standalone resource showcase, instructed Anna to composite final art for presentation screenshots, and rapidly built our itch.io page to tell the story of what Cosmic Catch could be.

[Screenshot:
Final Build]

I created a pitch video highlighting our complete art direction, music, and UX designs. Rather than presenting a broken prototype, we showcased a polished vision with professional presentation. The judges recognized the innovation in our concept and the maturity in how we handled adversity‚Äîawarding us Most Innovative despite the incomplete implementation.

What I Learned

Cosmic Catch taught me that a working prototype isn't just important‚Äîit's the foundation everything else builds on. Without that functional core, even beautiful art and music can't save a game. If I could rewind, I would demand a playable rhythm mechanic prototype within the first two hours, then expand from there.

[Retrospective:
Team Notes]

Team composition matters more than enthusiasm. I learned to look beyond "who likes the idea" to "who has the skills to execute under pressure." My friendly selections sometimes contributed to our struggles‚Äîa lesson in balancing personal relationships with project needs.

Most valuable was learning to stay calm when plans collapse. When you're exhausted and watching hours of work potentially go nowhere, panic helps no one. I discovered I could shift into damage control mode, make hard decisions quickly, and rally the team around a new goal. That composure under pressure has served me in every project since.

[Final Presentation:
Concept Showcase]

Looking ahead, I now approach rapid prototyping with a prototype-first mentality: prove the core mechanic works, then add features. I build project plans that prioritize one working system over multiple theoretical ones. And I've become much more strategic about team assembly‚Äînot just collecting talent, but understanding how personalities and skill levels mesh under deadline pressure.
