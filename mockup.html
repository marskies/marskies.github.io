Learn To Leap

Research-led redesign of a youth literacy platform · 2025

The Brief. Learn To Leap came to the team with a stalled growth curve and a hunch: their onboarding was losing parents before children ever opened the app. They wanted clarity, not a redesign for its own sake.

I led a six-week mixed-methods study across three age cohorts and two parent personas, pairing diary studies with moderated usability sessions. The first surprise was how often parents paused mid-onboarding to read aloud — a behavior the existing flow actively interrupted with autoplay videos.

What we found. Three friction patterns emerged across 24 sessions: opaque progress indicators, an account flow that asked for payment before any value was demonstrated, and a tone of voice that felt institutional rather than warm. None of these were visible in the analytics dashboard.

I rebuilt the onboarding around a "first win in ninety seconds" target and rewrote the empty states in a voice closer to a librarian than a marketing team. The case study walks through the artifacts: research plan, affinity wall, journey map, and the redesigned flow.

Where it landed. The redesign moved into a phased rollout in late 2025. Early signal: a 38% lift in completed onboardings and a measurable drop in support tickets tagged "confused / lost." More importantly, parents started telling us where they got stuck — which they hadn't before.

I grew up halfway between a sketchbook and a science fair. My dad taught me to ask "what's actually going on here?" before reaching for a solution; my mom taught me to make the answer beautiful. I think most of my work since then has just been trying to keep both of them in the room.

The first thing I ever designed that someone used was a color-coded chore wheel for my younger brother. He ignored it. That was, in hindsight, my first usability test.

These days I work in the seam between research and product — the place where a quote from a user has to turn into a wireframe by Friday. I like that pressure. I think it's where the most honest design lives.

Outside the work: I freedive (badly), make pottery (less badly), and keep a list of fonts that make me feel a specific kind of homesick. Ask me about it.
