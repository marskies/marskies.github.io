Introduction

[Booth Photo:
Convergence 2026]

Learn to Leap is a mixed-methods UX research study built around a custom Unity platformer that lets players choose how much the tutorial tells them. It investigates the gap between what players say they want from game tutorials and what they actually do behaviorally—and what that gap means for how designers approach onboarding.

Most platformer tutorials use a one-size-fits-all approach. Experienced players often find them patronizing and skip them entirely. Newer players sometimes find them inadequate and get lost. Existing research has focused mostly on whether tutorials successfully teach mechanics, or on adaptive systems that adjust automatically based on performance. Far less is known about player preference itself, and whether players accurately self-assess what they actually need.

[Unity Prototype:
The Double Jump Gap]

I started with a fairly straightforward hypothesis: that a player's gaming experience level would predict how much tutorial guidance they preferred. I expected experienced players to gravitate toward minimal guidance and newer players toward explicit guidance. The data had other plans.

This was my senior capstone at the Digital Worlds Institute, University of Florida, conducted under the advisement of Professor Darius Brown and Shelley Kaufeld and operating under IRB protocol ET00049273.

Final Piece

The final deliverable was a complete research study with a playable Unity prototype, a full thesis, a conference presentation at Convergence 2026, and a live data visualization tool that ran at the booth in real time.

[Three Tutorial
UI States]

The Unity prototype centered on a single core challenge: a gap that requires a double jump to cross. Players could toggle between three tutorial states at any time during gameplay, giving them complete control over the level of guidance they received.

The Three Tutorial States

Minimal

No on-screen guidance. Players figure out the double jump entirely on their own.

Guided

A brief text and visual hint suggests the mechanic without explicit instruction.

Explicit

Full instruction with visual indicators showing exactly where and when to jump.

[Live Data Viz:
Flask Booth Tool]

For the Convergence booth, I built a Python and Flask data visualization tool that displayed each new visitor's tutorial usage patterns alongside the original eleven study participants in real time. Visitors could see how their behavior compared to the research dataset immediately after playing—turning the booth itself into an extension of the study.

The full thesis will be published through the University of Florida Libraries.

Process

Methodology

[Research
Framework]

Eleven participants completed sessions that combined three data streams: automatic Unity telemetry capturing deaths, completion time, time spent in each tutorial state, and switch counts; a pre-play survey on gaming frequency; and a semi-structured post-play interview. Qualitative data was analyzed using Braun and Clarke's six-phase thematic analysis framework.

What the Data Showed

The original hypothesis was not supported. Gaming experience level did not reliably predict tutorial preference. Regular and frequent gamers were split across all three options, and the one participant who preferred Minimal was a frequent gamer who described enjoying the challenge of discovery.

But two more interesting findings emerged in the data. First, a clear say/do mismatch: players who stated a preference for more explicit guidance often spent the majority of their actual gameplay time in the Minimal state. Stated preference and behavioral usage were measuring two different things.

[Data Comparison:
Stated vs. Actual]

The Finding That Reframed Everything

Unanimous Result

Every single participant said the ability to choose their tutorial level was a positive part of the experience. Not a majority. Unanimous.

That finding reframed the study entirely. The question shifted from "which tutorial is best for which player" to "why do so few platformers offer players any choice at all." The most consistent thing in the data was not a preference for any specific tutorial style—it was a preference for having the choice in the first place.

What I Learned

The most valuable lesson was that a research project can outgrow its original question, and that is a feature, not a failure. The unexpected finding became the strongest contribution of the entire study. The hypothesis I started with would have produced a much less interesting thesis than the one the data actually wrote.

[Thesis Cover
or Award Photo]

I also learned a great deal about the discipline required to let data speak honestly, especially when it disproves a hypothesis I was personally invested in. There is a real temptation to soften an unexpected finding or frame it as partial support. Resisting that temptation is most of what makes research credible.

On the practical side, I learned that mixed methods work because the contrast between what people say and what they do is often where the real insight lives. Telemetry without interviews would have given me numbers without meaning. Interviews without telemetry would have produced a study that confidently confirmed a hypothesis the actual behavior contradicted.

[Booth Visitors
Playing Prototype]

Finally, I learned that research momentum is social. The moments I doubted the project most were the moments I most needed to talk about it, and the interest of classmates, friends, family, and peers was what carried me through the long middle stretch when the data was still raw and the story had not yet emerged. A solo project is rarely as solo as it looks on paper.
